A LONG ROAD

Medical providers and patients navigate the uneven terrain in a lengthy battle against access to proper health care.

In Appalachia, the rolling hills keep the people and the services they need at a distance. A daily routine could require extended periods of travel and unreliable cell phone coverage throughout. For those who do not have access to reliable transportation or may be condensed to the landscape of the region, a miniscule task can seem like a burden. Athens EMS knows the impact of these situations due to the constant risk it may have on a life they are trying to save.

RESPONDING TO EMERGENCIES
IN RURAL AREAS

Locating homes and finding their way to the scenes of accidents are just some of the difficulties The Athens County Emergency Medical Services (EMS) Station often faces on a daily basis. Chief of Athens County EMS, Rick Calebs, recounted the search for a victim of a logging accident in February 2020. While dispatch knew that the caller was located on Mountville Road, on the border of Athens and Morgan Counties, the precise location was difficult to find.

The ambulance only knew the road and it is a particularly long road, the ambulance did not know which end to enter and had nothing to go on."
Rick Calebs, the chief of Athens County Medical Services (ACMS)

The accident was not able to be seen from the road, and the EMTs had to begin traveling down the winding narrow road from one end, waiting to be frantically flagged down by the caller. According to Chief Calebs, this is an example of many incidents in which locating a caller is problematic. As he describes it, the time it takes for some callers to reach dispatch and the time spent in locating the scene of the accident can be the difference between life and death. It took 22 minutes for the EMTs to arrive at the location.

The Bureau of Workers’ Compensation is currently investigating as to whether or not the logging company, the employer of the victim, is a safe working environment. The victim of the accident did not survive. The victim of the accident did not survive.

It can take EMS anywhere from 10-30 minutes to reach a caller in Athens County, with the turnaround to a local hospital requiring 60-75 minutes.

Chief Calebs explained that inadequate cell phone coverage and unmarked roads are common issues encountered by his department when responding to emergency calls. Many regions in the county experience poor or no cell service, requiring that people stand in one area of their house, or even outside of their house, in order to call 911. Many of these roads are unmarked, increasing the level of difficulty in searching for callers. While county engineers work to update signage in the area, signs are often knocked down by snow plows, may be vandalized or disappear for unknown reasons.

It’s not as simple as you find in an urban area where they just give you a street address and you can go there."
Rick Calebs, the chief of ACMS

To reach a lot of these unmarked locations, EMT drivers use different forms of GPS systems like Google and Apple maps. When these systems fail due to service issues or even lack of information about the location, drivers are able to call back to an 911 operator, to have them help locate the caller.

Common issues for which Athens EMS receives calls include shortness of breath, chest pain, lung problems, heart problems and long-term health issues. Southeast Ohio experiences disproportionally high levels of illnesses such as depression, heart disease, and diabetes. This health crisis has gained the attention of health professionals in the area, who are seeking solutions that are accessible and reliable for patients.

MOBILE HEALTH CLINIC

Sherri Oliver is the executive director for Community Health Programs and Area Health Education Center. In this role, Oliver helps run a free clinic on Ohio University’s campus through the Heritage College of Osteopathic Medicine, along with two mobile health units that serve 24 counties in Southeastern and Eastern Ohio. The mobile health units treat patients that otherwise would not be able to afford a visit to the doctor. As of April 2020, the mobile health trucks have suspended their operations due to COVID-19.

“The mobile health units are essentially “doctors’ offices on wheels,” Oliver explained. The trucks are 40-feet long, with two exam rooms and a waiting room. Nurse practitioners, medical students and registered nurses all travel in the mobile health unit to counties to provide medical care through various health clinics. These units present an opportunity for the Heritage College students to gain hands-on experience, while interfacing with real clients and providing a much needed service.

“We see people who don’t have access to transportation or don’t have access to reliable transportation,” Oliver said. The availability of the mobile health unit vehicles through the Heritage College are one way in which regional leaders are seeking creative solutions to the protracted health issues of Southeast Ohio’s residents. For some, the availability of these services has become a part of their annual, or even monthly, routine.

David Lee Pannell, 63, lives in Marietta, Ohio and visits the mobile health unit once a month in Coolville, Ohio. Pannell is diagnosed with diabetes and needs the free care and prescriptions that the unit provides, without these he would not be able to live a normal life. Having a free mobile health clinic available for Pannell has made the idea of being healthy and cared for more achievable, without the stress of additional bills.

Without the help of these nurses and this mobile unit, I without a doubt would be 6 feet under...this truck has saved my life."
Dave, mobile clinic patient

Once a month, Pannell and his wife Kathleen wake up at their home in Marietta, load up their car and make the half an hour drive along the Ohio River to Coolville. This is the closest unit location, and without it the Pannells would have to drive an hour to Athens for a 20 minute health checkup. Pannell has been actively seeking care at the mobile health unit for over three years, and plans to continue for as long as he can. For Dave, the truck’s services are what keep him alive.

ECONOMIC ACCESS

While community leaders are working hard to ensure access to care for all, innovative programs like the mobile health units cannot address every need. “But sometimes we could take that medical health unit within maybe two miles of your house and if you don’t have a vehicle to get to us, sometimes we’re still not totally meeting that transportation gap,” Oliver described.

In order to meet this gap, Oliver finds herself and her colleagues at the Community Health Program using grants or community donations to give people gas cards in order to help them travel to their health clinics.

“We see a lot of people who have health insurance, but their health insurance has such a high deductible, that they can’t really use it,” Oliver said. “So, they’ll still come to the free clinic and they’ll still seek out our services because they can’t access health care in a traditional setting.”

When the uninsured or underinsured are unable to access care, the worlds of Sheri Oliver and Chief Calebs can collide. Calebs says that some community members may not call an ambulance because they cannot afford it. When these uninsured members of the community do not see doctors regularly, their health issues can snowball into more protracted problems and lead to more intense situations for EMS. In addition, once they enter the health care system, the costs for care are higher than the initial visit would have been because their conditions are so much worse.

I think that's a huge issue that we have to deal with as a society because some people view healthcare as a right, other people are adamant that it's not a right”
Rick Calebs, Chief of ACMS

HOCKING ATHENS PERRY
COUNTY ACTION

IIn non-emergency cases, Athens County residents have access to a cheaper mode of transportation through the Hocking Athens Perry Community Action (HAPCAP). HAPCAP has been working to improve the community’s quality of life in the Tri-County area since the early 1960’s.

One of the five divisions of the nonprofit’s focus is transportation. In HAPCAP’s transit office, Athens and Hocking County mobility manager, Jessie Schmitzer, plays a key role in managing the services that HAPCAP offers. “Athens On Demand Transit is a door to door service that services all of Athens county and services anybody for any reason,” Schmitzer said. Originally provided for senior citizens and individuals with disabilities, as of Jan. 1, 2020, HAPAP has opened Athens On Demand for use by anyone, for the cost of $2 one way.

Medical access...it's all encompassing. So getting to the hospital, getting to the doctor, especially if it's a recurring appointment, that makes it really difficult to find that transportation constantly,” Schmitzer said."
Jessie Schmitzer, Athens and Hocking County mobility manager

“I'm seeing a lot of people that might have recurring appointments that they can get transportation to sometimes, but not all the time,” Schmitzer said. “A big reason for that is because our transportation programs are really at capacity because there's such a huge need.”

The high demand for rides through HAPCAP managed transportation programs can be an issue for riders banking on the program as their ticket to the doctor’s office. Schmitzer said that once someone knows when their next appointment is, they should call right away, in order to reserve a ride in time.

“So we got together with Athens on Demand Transit and Green Cab, and came up with this really cool collaboration. If someone is stranded at the emergency room, then OhioHealth staff can call either Athens On Demand Transit or Green Cab, depending on the time of day, and they could go pick them up at the cost of OhioHealth and then bring them back home,” Schmitzer said.

COMMUNITY AND COVID-19

A lternative long-distance medical transport options are available for some residents of Southeast Ohio. Paula Peterson, of Albany Ohio, feels satisfied with her two medical care services: Hopewell and PASSPORT. Peterson is a disabled senior citizen, and is able to receive transportation through both of these services.

After being injured in 2008 while working as a nurse’s aide, Peterson had back surgery for a fractured vertebrae and discovered severe scoliosis. Through PASSPORT, a waiver program administered through Ohio Medicaid, Peterson is assigned a nurse case manager and receives home health aide two days a week.

“They've really helped me out a lot,” Peterson said. “If I've got to go even as far as Columbus to the doctors, PASSPORT will pay for it.”

“I'm doing pretty well, thanks be to God,” Peterson said.

Peterson is also doing well despite the spread of COVID-19. Spending time with her grandchildren is what Peterson misses the most. “I'm doing really great, and I don't see why that couldn't continue.” Peterson said she is pretty well isolated to begin with, so the COVID-19 pandemic has not changed her life much. She washes her hands more and disinfects her apartment once a week.

Although this time is mostly stress free for Peterson, there is a concern for her daughter-in-law. “She's in the emergency room right now and they're testing her for Corona. So that's stressing me out.” Peterson uses FaceTime to talk to her family during

“I'm really not terribly worried about it,” Peterson said. “My family has been really good about staying away since everything went to lock down.”

If I get it, I get it, and there's nothing I can do about that except go to the hospital and God’s will be done.”
Paula Peterson, Albany resident

In the past month, some have raised concerns about the availability of coronavirus tests to Southeast Ohio due to the lack of healthcare access in the area.

In response to these concerns, Ohio House Rep, Jay Edwards, R-Nelsonville, has stated that the area is not running low on test kits. Edwards says that Southeast Ohio is “on par per capita with other areas of the state.”

OhioHealth O'Bleness Hospital has begun preparation for possible COVID-19 surges by erecting emergency “triage tents” outside of the hospital in the Emergency Department. At the moment, the tents are prepared as a precautionary measure. Spread of COVID-19 in the community has not yet reached a level that requires patient triage.

Two tents are set up and a third will soon accompany them. Each tent will serve a different purpose. Patients are to be evaluated at the first tent and depending on the physician’s determination the patient will go into a tent for definite COVID-19 or a tent for possible COVID-19.

These tents are not for coronavirus testing.
